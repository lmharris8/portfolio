Research Interests

My research interests are grounded in the relationship between interior environments and human behavior, with particular attention to social engagement, equity, and everyday lived experience. As an undergraduate at Samford University, I completed an honors anthropology course that encouraged me to examine my field through an anthropological lens. In one case study, I explored the evolution of residential kitchen design alongside the rise of feminism, revealing how domestic interiors reflect shifting social structures, gender roles, and patterns of engagement. This early work prompted a sustained interest in the intersection of interior design and social behavior.

Building on these ideas, my master's thesis investigated how educational facility design influences adolescent social interaction. My research demonstrated that social development plays a critical role in adolescent health and well-being, highlighting the need for intentional interior design strategies that support meaningful social engagement within educational environments. This work serves as the foundation for my ongoing scholarly interests at the intersection of interior design, psychology, and sociology, with an emphasis on evidence-based approaches to human-centered environments.

I believe research in interior design should bridge academic theory and professional practice while remaining socially relevant and accessible. I am particularly interested in design inquiry that addresses everyday experience, including small-scale residential and community-focused environments that serve individuals and families beyond large institutional or high-budget contexts. I view this work as an opportunity to connect scholarship, teaching, and professional practice in ways that support equity, ethical responsibility, and meaningful impact.
